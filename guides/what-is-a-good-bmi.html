What is a good BMI?

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a quick screening number that compares your weight to your height. For most adults, a BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is classed as a healthy weight — but the number comes with important caveats worth understanding.

The standard adult categories

  • Below 18.5 — Underweight
  • 18.5 to 24.9 — Healthy weight
  • 25.0 to 29.9 — Overweight
  • 30.0 and above — Obese

How BMI is calculated

BMI is your weight in kilograms divided by the square of your height in metres (kg/m²). For example, someone 1.75 m tall weighing 70 kg has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) ≈ 22.9 — comfortably in the healthy range.

The big limitation

BMI doesn't distinguish muscle from fat, or where fat is stored. A very muscular athlete can register as "overweight" despite low body fat, while someone with a "healthy" BMI might carry excess abdominal fat. It also doesn't account for age, sex, ethnicity or bone density. That's why doctors use BMI as a starting screen, not a diagnosis, and often combine it with waist measurement and other indicators.

So is BMI useful?

Yes — at a population level and as a simple personal check it's genuinely helpful and costs nothing to calculate. Just treat it as one data point. If you're concerned about your weight or health, a doctor can give context BMI alone can't.

FAQ

Is BMI different for men and women?

The adult categories are the same, though body composition differs. Children and teens use age- and sex-specific percentiles instead.

What's a healthy BMI for older adults?

Some research suggests a slightly higher BMI may be protective in older age. Discuss your situation with a doctor.
