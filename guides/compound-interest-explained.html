Compound interest, explained

Compound interest is interest earned on your interest. It's the engine behind long-term wealth — and, on the flip side, the reason credit-card debt spirals. Once you understand it, a lot of money decisions get clearer.

Simple vs compound interest

Simple interest pays only on your original deposit. Put €1,000 at 10% simple interest and you earn €100 every year — forever €100. Compound interest pays on the original deposit plus all the interest already added. Year one you earn €100; year two you earn 10% of €1,100 = €110; year three, 10% of €1,210 = €121, and so on. The growth accelerates.

The formula

A = P · (1 + r/n)^(n·t)

  • A = final amount
  • P = principal (starting amount)
  • r = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
  • n = times interest compounds per year
  • t = number of years

Why starting early beats saving more

Here's the part that surprises people. Suppose two savers each earn 7% a year:

  • Anna invests €200/month from age 25 to 35 (10 years), then stops and never adds another cent.
  • Ben waits, then invests €200/month from age 35 all the way to 65 (30 years).

Anna contributed €24,000; Ben contributed €72,000 — three times as much. Yet by age 65 Anna often ends up with a similar or larger balance, purely because her money had an extra decade to compound. Time in the market is the most powerful variable.

The same force works against debt

Credit cards compound interest on unpaid balances — often monthly, at 20%+ APR. The same snowball that grows savings grows debt, which is why paying off high-interest debt is one of the best guaranteed "returns" you can get.

FAQ

How often should interest compound?

More frequent compounding (monthly vs annually) helps, but the difference is small compared to the rate and the time horizon.

Does this account for inflation?

No — these are nominal figures. To estimate real purchasing power, subtract expected inflation from your rate.
