How to calculate loan payments

Whether it's a mortgage, a car loan or a personal loan, a fixed-rate loan is repaid in equal monthly instalments. Each payment covers the interest charged that month plus a slice of the principal. Here's exactly how that monthly figure is worked out.

The formula

The standard amortizing-loan payment formula is:

M = P · r · (1 + r)ⁿ / ((1 + r)ⁿ − 1)

  • M = the monthly payment you're solving for
  • P = the principal (the amount borrowed)
  • r = the monthly interest rate = annual rate ÷ 12
  • n = the total number of payments = years × 12

A worked example

Say you borrow €20,000 at a 6.5% annual rate over 5 years:

  • P = 20,000
  • r = 0.065 ÷ 12 = 0.0054167
  • n = 5 × 12 = 60

Plugging those in gives a monthly payment of about €391.32. Over 60 months that's €23,479 total — meaning roughly €3,479 in interest on top of the €20,000 you borrowed.

Why early payments are mostly interest

Interest each month is charged on the remaining balance. Early on, the balance is high, so most of your payment goes to interest. As the balance shrinks, more of each fixed payment chips away at the principal. This is why making extra payments early in a loan saves so much — every extra euro goes straight to principal and removes all the future interest it would have generated.

Three ways to pay less interest

  • Shorten the term. A 15-year mortgage costs far less interest than a 30-year one, even though the monthly payment is higher.
  • Get a lower rate. Even half a percent adds up to thousands over a long loan.
  • Overpay when you can. Lump sums and small monthly extras both shorten the loan.

FAQ

Does this include taxes and insurance?

No — the formula covers principal and interest only. For a mortgage, add property tax and insurance separately.

What if the rate is 0%?

Then the payment is simply the principal divided by the number of months.
